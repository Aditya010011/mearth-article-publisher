Project Moon Hut Classification System Structure
Introduction: Understanding the Project Moon Hut Classification System
The Project Moon Hut Classification System is not a taxonomy of industries, technologies, or functions. It’s a 
geographic lens—a new way to define “where” in the expanding Mearth (Moon + Earth) ecosystem.
Unlike models that treat “space” as a sector—which it is not—this system reframes the conversation around 
geography. It defines a continuum of 15 operational zones, stretching from the surface of the Earth to regions 
well beyond Mars. Each zone reflects different physical, logistical, biological, economic, and technological 
conditions. This isn’t a metaphor—it’s a map of where real work happens.
Why this matters:
Most people use the term “space” as if it’s a singular place—that mysterious “out there.” Which would be 
the same as when asked, “Where do you work?” you’d hear the reply, “Earth.” But there’s a vast difference 
between operating in Low Earth Orbit, “deploying infrastructure between the Earth and the Moon, working on
the Moon, or Mars and Phobos. Yet until now, no shared framework existed to define these distinctions.
Some might argue the system is too simple for the complexity of our solar system. [A conversation for 
another article.] But any classification model must serve its purpose. Ours is focused on the Mearth™
ecosystem—where the vast majority of meaningful development will occur over the next 40 years. It creates 
a foundational layer for building the Mearth Ecosystem and Mearth Economic System enabling 
multidimensional coordination.
The Classification System serves three purposes:
1. Clarity — It helps the general public, stakeholders, and decision-makers understand what part of 
the Mearth ecosystem a conversation, investment, innovation, etc. one refers to—or what conditions 
innovators are addressing when working beyond Earth.
2. Alignment — It gives contributors and collaborators a shared reference when navigating platform 
development, tech transfer, immersive design, education systems, database structure, and 
opportunity discovery.
3. Coordination — It enables distributed teams to align timelines, structure workflows, allocate 
resources, and visualize system interdependencies—built on common geographic terms instead of 
assumptions.
You’ll find three tiers of explanations below for each region (Zones 1–15):
• Basic — for a curious 15-year-old or someone new
• Slightly Technical — for someone looking to understand more
• Highly Technical — for experienced professionals, researchers, or advanced planners

Earth
1. Basic Definition: Earth is the planet where we live, complete with land, water, and air. It's the third 
planet from the sun.
2. Slightly Technical Definition: Earth is a terrestrial planet that orbits the Sun at an average distance 
of about 93 million miles. It features a varied environment, including oceans, forests, and deserts, 
which support a wide range of life.
3. Highly Technical Definition: Earth is an oblate spheroid with an equatorial radius of approximately 
6,378 kilometers and a polar radius of about 6,357 kilometers. It is situated in the Solar System and 
orbits the Sun at an average distance of roughly 1 astronomical unit (AU), which equates to about 
149.6 million kilometers. The Earth's core consists predominantly of an inner solid core and an outer 
liquid core, primarily composed of iron and nickel. The mantle, rich in silicates, undergoes 
convection that drives plate tectonics. Above the mantle is the Earth's crust, a layer of varying 
composition that supports the hydrosphere and atmosphere. Earth has a complex magnetic field 
generated by dynamo action in its core, and its axial tilt of approximately 23.5 degrees results in the 
planet's seasonal variations. The planet's atmosphere is composed mainly of nitrogen and oxygen 
and plays a vital role in sustaining life by moderating temperature, blocking harmful solar radiation, 
and contributing to the carbon and water cycles.
Atmosphere
1. Basic Definition: The Earth's atmosphere is the layer of air that surrounds our planet. It's what we 
breathe and what keeps our planet warm enough to support life.
2. Slightly Technical Definition: The Earth's atmosphere is a mixture of gases that envelops the Earth, 
composed primarily of nitrogen and oxygen. It extends from the surface of the Earth up to about 620 
miles (1,000 km). The atmosphere is responsible for climate regulation, and it's divided into layers 
based on temperature variations: troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, and thermosphere.
3. Highly Technical Definition: Earth's atmosphere is a stratified mixture of gases held in place by 
gravity, with a composition of about 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, and trace amounts of other elements 
and compounds like argon, carbon dioxide, and water vapor. It has a complex layered structure 
divided by temperature gradients. The lowest layer, the troposphere, extends from the Earth's 
surface up to about 8-15 km, depending on latitude, and is characterized by a decrease in 
temperature with altitude. The stratosphere, ranging from about 15-50 km, contains the ozone layer 
and exhibits an increase in temperature with altitude. The mesosphere extends from 50-85 km, 
followed by the thermosphere, which can reach up to 600 km, where interactions with solar radiation 
cause ionization to occur. Atmospheric pressure decreases exponentially with altitude, and its 
thermal profile is influenced by a variety of factors, including solar radiation absorption, infrared 
radiation emission, and adiabatic processes. The atmosphere plays a critical role in various 
geophysical cycles, such as the water cycle and carbon cycle, and provides a shield against solar 
radiation and meteoroid entry.
Low Earth Orbit (LEO)
1. Basic Definition: Low Earth Orbit is the area in space that's closest to Earth. Satellites and the 
International Space Station are often in Low Earth Orbit, which is a few hundred miles above the 
Earth's surface.
2. Slightly Technical Definition: Low Earth Orbit (LEO) refers to orbits around Earth at an altitude 
ranging from approximately 200 to 2,000 kilometers (124 to 1,242 miles). Objects in Low Earth Orbit
travel at speeds of about 7.5 km/s to 8 km/s. This is the most commonly used orbit for satellite 
launches, including communication and Earth-observing satellites, due to lower energy 
requirements for both launch and orbital maintenance.

3. Highly Technical Definition: Low Earth Orbit is an orbital regime situated at an altitude typically 
between 200 km and 2,000 km above Earth's mean sea level, featuring orbital periods ranging from 
about 88 to 128 minutes. In this zone, orbital decay due to atmospheric drag is significant, 
necessitating periodic reboost maneuvers for satellites and other space platforms to maintain 
orbital stability. The orbital velocity required to sustain a stable Low Earth Orbit is approximately 7.5 
to 8 km/s, depending on the specific altitude and inclination. Given its proximity to Earth, LEO is 
heavily affected by Earth's gravitational field and experiences minimal influence from the 
gravitational perturbations of other celestial bodies. This orbit is prime real estate for Earth 
observation missions, scientific experiments in microgravity, and telecommunication networks. 
Advanced calculations for objects in LEO must also take into account effects such as the Earth's 
oblateness, solar and lunar gravitational forces, and the gravitational interactions with other 
celestial bodies, as well as the influences of Earth's magnetic field on charged particles.
Medium Earth Orbit (MEO)
1. Basic Definition: Medium Earth Orbit is higher than Low Earth Orbit but closer than High Earth Orbit. 
This is where some satellites, like those used for GPS, are usually found. It's a few thousand miles 
above Earth.
2. Slightly Technical Definition: Medium Earth Orbit (MEO) ranges from an altitude of approximately 
2,000 to 35,786 kilometers (1,243 to 22,236 miles) above Earth. Satellites in Medium Earth Orbit
complete their orbit in a few hours and are often used for navigation, weather monitoring, and 
regional communication. These orbits are higher than Low Earth Orbit but lower than Geostationary 
Orbit, offering a balance between orbital stability and signal latency.
3. Highly Technical Definition: Medium Earth Orbit is situated between 2,000 km and 35,786 km 
above Earth's mean sea level, with orbital periods that range from several hours to just under one 
day. MEO satellites often inhabit specific orbital planes to optimize coverage and are generally 
characterized by lower launch and operational costs compared to Geostationary Orbits. MEO is 
particularly advantageous for applications requiring a compromise between high-data-rate 
transmission and low-latency communication. Given their altitude, MEO satellites are less affected 
by Earth's atmospheric drag but are still subject to orbital decay over extended periods. Precise 
station-keeping and orbital maneuvers are required to mitigate the long-term effects of gravitational 
perturbations from the Moon and the Sun, as well as the Earth's oblateness. The medium orbital 
altitude allows for higher data throughput and lower latency compared to higher orbits but requires 
more complex ground station tracking systems due to faster orbital movement relative to the Earth's 
surface.
High Earth Orbit (HEO)
1. Basic Definition: High Earth Orbit is farthest from the Earth among the three main types of Earth 
orbits. It's where you'd find some weather satellites and scientific observatories. The satellites here 
move more slowly and have a longer orbit time around the Earth.
2. Slightly Technical Definition: High Earth Orbit (HEO) is an orbital region located at altitudes above 
35,786 kilometers (22,236 miles). Satellites in High Earth Orbit often have long orbital periods, 
extending from about a day to a year. They are commonly used for weather observation, scientific 
research, and certain communication applications.
3. Highly Technical Definition: High Earth Orbit extends from altitudes above 35,786 km to the edge of 
Earth's gravitational sphere of influence, approximately at a distance of 1.5 million km (about 0.01 
Astronomical Units). HEOs have orbital periods ranging from 24 hours to one year and are 
characterized by a larger semi-major axis and lower orbital velocity compared to LEO and MEO. 
These orbits are frequently chosen for high-sensitivity astronomical observatories, space weather 
monitoring systems, and specialized communication satellites that require expansive coverage 
areas or highly eccentric orbits. While satellites in HEO are less affected by atmospheric drag, they

The Space Between High Earth Orbit and the Moon's Orbit
1. Basic Definition: This is the empty space where there's not much other than a few satellites and 
probes. It's the gap between where most of our satellites hang out and where the Moon orbits Earth.
2. Slightly Technical Definition: This region, often referred to as "cis-lunar space," spans the distance 
from High Earth Orbit, starting at around 35,786 kilometers above Earth, to the Moon's orbit at about 
384,400 kilometers. This space is mostly a vacuum and is where missions to the Moon traverse.
3. Highly Technical Definition: The interlunar region, colloquially known as "cis-lunar space," extends 
from the edge of the geostationary orbit (GEO) at approximately 35,786 kilometers to the Moon's 
semi-major axis at an average distance of 384,400 kilometers from Earth. This region is 
characterized by negligible atmospheric particles, requiring spacecraft to utilize onboard propulsion 
systems for trajectory corrections. The space is influenced by both Earth’s and the Moon’s 
gravitational fields, and encompasses the second Lagrange point (L2). It serves as a critical passage 
for translunar injection maneuvers in missions aimed at Moon exploration or beyond. Various 
perturbations like solar radiation pressure, Earth’s gravitational potential harmonics, and lunar 
gravitational anomalies must be taken into account for precise orbital insertions and mission 
planning in this regime.
Orbit Around the Moon
1. Basic Definition: The Moon has a very thin atmosphere and no natural satellites. Anything orbiting 
the Moon would be directly affected by the Moon's weak gravitational pull.
2. Slightly Technical Definition: The Moon's atmosphere, also known as an exosphere, is extremely 
thin and virtually non-existent from a practical standpoint. It lacks the layers and composition found 
in Earth's atmosphere. Satellites or objects in orbit around the Moon are essentially in a nearvacuum, with negligible atmospheric drag.
3. Highly Technical Definition: The Moon's atmosphere, often referred to as an exosphere, is 
composed of trace amounts of elements like helium, neon, and argon. Its density is so low that it 
cannot support aerodynamic flight. The exosphere extends up to an altitude where the mean free 
path of a particle is comparable to the scale height, making it virtually collisionless. Any object in 
lunar orbit would have to contend with perturbations primarily from gravitational interactions with 
the Earth and the Sun, rather than any effects of the Moon's atmosphere. Lunar orbits are also 
influenced by mass concentrations ("mascons") within the Moon, which cause gravitational 
anomalies affecting orbital stability. Given the absence of a substantial atmosphere, there is no 
aerodynamic drag to slow down objects in orbit, requiring active orbital adjustments to maintain a 
stable trajectory.
The Moon

planet. It's the celestial body where astronauts first landed in 1969.
2. Slightly Technical Definition: The Moon is a rocky celestial body, about 1/6th the size of Earth, 
orbiting at an average distance of 384,400 kilometers. It's tidally locked with Earth, meaning the

3. Highly Technical Definition: The Moon is Earth's only natural satellite with a mean radius of 1,737.1

limited longitudinal and latitudinal oscillations in the Moon's orientation. Its regolith surface layer 
has been extensively studied for insights into its composition and the history of impacts on the lunar 
surface. The Moon is also subject to Earth's Hill sphere, within which it exerts its own gravitational

The Mearth Line
1. Basic Definition: The Mearth Line is an imaginary sphere with a 297,000-mile radius around Earth 
and the Moon. It includes Earth's atmosphere, the Moon's orbit, and even the space in between. 
Think of it as the boundary that defines what's considered the Moon-Earth system or "Mearth," 
including anything like spaceships or satellites within this sphere.
2. Slightly Technical Definition: The Mearth Line is a conceptual boundary delineated by a sphere with 
a radius of 297,000 miles (478,499 kilometers). This sphere includes Earth's atmospheric layers, the 
Moon's orbital path, and its near-nonexistent atmosphere. The Mearth Line is a geographical 
framework aimed to capture the complex gravitational interactions and orbital dance between Earth 
and the Moon. It encapsulates not just static celestial bodies, but dynamic entities like satellites and 
spacecraft traversing between Earth and the Moon.
3. Highly Technical Definition: The Mearth Line is a theoretical boundary defined by a sphere centered 
on the Moon-Earth barycenter with a radius of 297,000 miles (478,499 kilometers) or a diameter of 
594,000 miles (956,998 kilometers). It encompasses Earth's multi-layered atmosphere, including the 
exosphere, and extends to include the Moon's semi-synchronous, elliptical orbital path and its 
vestigial atmosphere. This construct facilitates a more nuanced understanding of celestial 
mechanics, particularly the gravitational forces at play between Earth and the Moon. The boundary is 
dynamic, capable of including trans-lunar and cis-lunar spacecraft, satellites, and any stations 
operating within this sphere. In essence, the Mearth Line serves as a demarcation for classifying 
space-based activities and celestial bodies as being part of the interconnected Moon-Earth system, 
or "Mearth."
The Space Between the Moon's Orbit and Mars's Atmosphere/Orbit
1. Basic Definition: This space is an area of the solar system that lies beyond the Moon but before 
reaching Mars. It's essentially a vast vacuum with some asteroids and cosmic dust.
2. Slightly Technical Definition: This region of space can be characterized as interplanetary space 
lying between the spheres of influence of Earth's moon and Mars. It encompasses the Asteroid Belt 
and experiences solar wind from the Sun. It is devoid of atmospheric interference and largely defined 
by gravitational dynamics between celestial bodies.
3. Highly Technical Definition: This segment of interplanetary space exists within the heliocentric 
frame and is largely dominated by solar radiation pressure, gravitational forces exerted by the Sun, 
and micrometeoroid flux. The region also contains the Main Belt asteroids situated between the 
orbits of Mars and Jupiter, as well as transient objects such as Near-Earth Asteroids (NEAs) that can 
cross Earth's orbit. The lack of any appreciable atmosphere means that traditional aerodynamic 
principles are irrelevant here. Instead, celestial mechanics and orbital resonance become the 
primary factors affecting the motion of objects.

Atmosphere/Orbit around Mars
1. Basic Definition: Mars has a thin atmosphere and an orbit that goes around the Sun. This zone 
around Mars is where its moons and some human-made satellites circle the planet.
2. Slightly Technical Definition: The Martian atmosphere is primarily composed of carbon dioxide, 
with traces of nitrogen and argon, and its exosphere extends several hundred kilometers above the 
surface. The Martian orbit is an elliptical path around the Sun with an average distance of about 1.5 
AU (Astronomical Units).
3. Highly Technical Definition: Mars' orbit around the Sun is characterized by a semi-major axis of 
approximately 1.524 AU, an eccentricity of about 0.0934, and an orbital period of 686.971 Earth 
days. The Martian atmosphere has a scale height of about 11.1 km and experiences significant 
seasonal variations in pressure due to sublimation and condensation of its polar ice caps. The 
Martian exosphere, extending up to approximately 200 km above the surface, is the transition zone 
where molecular collisions become rarefied and where the influence of the solar wind becomes 
significant. It's within this exosphere that Mars' two irregularly-shaped moons, Phobos and Deimos, 
orbit, influenced by tidal gravitational forces exerted by Mars itself and the Sun’s gravitational 
perturbations.
Mars
1. Basic Definition: Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun in our solar system. It's often called the 'Red 
Planet' because of its reddish appearance, which is due to iron oxide or rust on its surface.
2. Slightly Technical Definition: Mars is an inner planet with a thin atmosphere primarily made of 
carbon dioxide. It has polar ice caps made of water and dry ice, ancient river valleys, and large 
impact craters. Its average distance from the Sun is about 141 million miles (227 million km), and it 
has a rotation period close to that of Earth (about 24.6 hours).
3. Highly Technical Definition: Mars is an oblate spheroid with an equatorial radius of approximately 
3,389.5 km and a polar radius of about 3,376.2 km. Its mean density is around 3.93 g/cm³, making it 
less dense than Earth. The planet exhibits significant geological diversity, including the largest 
volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons, and the longest canyon, Valles Marineris. Its thin 
atmosphere is mostly CO2, with surface pressures ranging from 30 Pa at the peak of Olympus Mons 
to over 1,155 Pa in Hellas Planitia. It has two small moons, Phobos and Deimos, which are likely 
captured asteroids or Kuiper Belt objects.
Deimos
1. Basic Definition: Deimos is one of the two moons orbiting Mars. It is the smaller and more distant of 
the two, and it appears smooth and featureless from Earth-based telescopes.
2. Slightly Technical Definition: Deimos is an irregularly shaped moon with dimensions of 
approximately 15 × 12.4 × 11 km. It is primarily composed of carbonaceous material and is thought 
to be a captured asteroid. It orbits Mars at an average distance of about 23,460 km (14,580 miles).
3. Highly Technical Definition: Deimos is an irregularly-shaped, non-spherical moon with a mean 
radius of about 6.2 km. Its orbit has a semi-major axis of approximately 23,460 km and an 
eccentricity of 0.00033, making it nearly circular. The moon's rotational period is synchronous with
its orbital period, which is about 30.3 hours. Spectroscopic analysis suggests its composition is 
similar to Type I or II carbonaceous chondrites. The moon's low albedo (0.068) suggests a surface 
covered in regolith, produced by micrometeorite impacts and space weathering.

1. Basic Definition: Phobos is the larger of the two moons orbiting Mars and is closer to the planet. It's 
known for its irregular shape and a large crater named Stickney.
2. Slightly Technical Definition: Phobos has dimensions of about 27 × 22 × 18 km and orbits Mars at 
an exceptionally close distance, averaging around 9,378 km (5,827 miles) from the Martian center. It 
is thought to be a captured asteroid or a remnant of Mars itself.
3. Highly Technical Definition: Phobos is an irregularly-shaped moon with a mean radius of 
approximately 11.1 km. It is in a near-circular orbit with a semi-major axis of 9,378 km and an 
eccentricity of 0.0151. The moon has a synchronous rotation, with an orbital period of about 7.66 
hours, which is less than a Martian day. Its surface composition resembles that of D-type asteroids, 
suggesting a potential captured origin. The moon is gradually spiraling towards Mars due to tidal 
deceleration, and it is expected to either crash into Mars or break apart within 50 million years. 
Spectral data indicates a surface composition rich in carbonaceous chondrite, and it has a low 
albedo of around 0.071.
Beyond 
1. Basic Definition: This category is a catch-all that includes everything else in the Universe not previously 
described—from our Sun and the inner planets Mercury and Venus to far-off galaxies, and the mysterious 
stretches of the Universe we've yet to explore. 
2. Slightly Technical Definition: Beyond the scope of our immediate Solar System and not otherwise 
categorized, this sector includes the inner rocky planets Mercury and Venus, our central star—the 
Sun—as well as extra-solar planetary systems (exoplanets), nebulae, black holes, quasars, and
galaxies. This sector is an amalgamation of all celestial bodies and phenomena, inclusive of those 
governed by complex astrophysics and characterized by diverse geophysical, atmospheric, and 
electromagnetic conditions.
3. Very Technical Definition: This ambit encompasses not just the local, but the cosmological. Within 
our Solar System, it integrates the high-radiation environments of Mercury and the greenhouse 
effect-ridden Venus, as well as our G-type main-sequence star, the Sun, characterized by its 
thermonuclear fusion of hydrogen into helium. On an interstellar level, this domain includes 
exoplanetary systems with their own Goldilocks zones, as well as relativistic phenomena such as 
black holes, where gravitational fields manifest extreme spacetime curvature. Extending even further 
to a galactic and intergalactic scale, this realm includes vast conglomerates of stars, dark matter, 
and interstellar medium that compose galaxies, along with large-scale structures such as galaxy 
clusters, superclusters, and the cosmic web. Importantly, this sector is the subject of unresolved 
questions about the nature of dark energy, the cosmic microwave background, and the ultimate fate 
of the Universe in cosmological timeframes. 
* Exclusion Note: The Sun, Mercury, and Venus are deliberately omitted from the Project Moon Hut

development plans, which suggests that human habitation or significant infrastructural development on 
these celestial bodies or further out into the solar system is unlikely within the timeframe.
